Action research

Action research is generally undertaken by practitioners in order to help develop their practice, although it can also be research with practitioners. The aim of this approach is to change practice by problem solving, which normally requires an intervention into the proceedings on the part of the researcher or practitioner. It usually begins with small changes that one single person can implement and then, if these are successful, the changes become gradually more extensive and widespread.

Case study

Case studies entail the detailed and intensive analysis of a single case: this could be a programme, event, organisation, phenomenon or one or more individuals. It is important to be clear about the boundaries of the case. The researcher collects data using a variety of collection methods over a period of time to shed light onto the case.

Experimental design

Experimental designs allow researchers to not only observe events, but also to control and even manipulate the conditions associated with them in order to find out what happens. This enables researchers to deliberately introduce a predetermined factor and measure the differences that result from the intervention while controlling for all other factors that might influence the outcome.

Ethnography

Ethnographic studies involve collecting data over a period of time through immersion in the field. This is usually to study the culture of a setting, behaviours, structures and values. The researcher acts as a participant observer in a natural setting. By becoming integrated into their field of research, they have opportunities to understand what takes place there and an increasing understanding of which data is relevant to collect to help understand what is observable.

Longitudinal studies and surveys

Longitudinal studies collect data over an extended period of time, with the data being repeatedly collected on a sample (of people, documents, etc.) on at least two occasions, but sometimes over many years. A good example of this would be the Child of our Time BBC documentary series, which plans to run for 20 years, following its 25 subjects from their birth in 1999 or 2000.

Surveying by interview or questionnaire is useful in gathering information from a wide range of people to look for patterns, as well as potential trends about particular phenomena or issues.

Narrative and biographical approaches

Narrative research encourages participants to tell their own story, in their own words, rather than answering a set of questions devised by researchers. Biographical research is a particular form of narrative research in which participants are encouraged to see their experiences in the context of their lives as a whole, with researchers seeking to understand the diverse influences that have shaped people’s lives. Narrative and biographical approaches explore how different actors involved in a particular situation or process actually experience it, and therefore provide rich evidence that can contribute to the development of more appropriate policies and practices.

Critical discourse analysis

Critical discourse analysis is the study of language in use in the world. It explores how language is used to not only say things, but also to actually do things in the world. As a research design, it focuses on texts – written, spoken or visual – and the relationships between language and power and is interested in how texts reproduce inequalities. Discourse analysis conceptualises language as constitutive of social practice. Thus, words we use inscribe our reality as much as they describe. They construct a particular version of the world. Power is understood as the ability of a social group to set the material conditions within which individuals as members of social groups live, learn and think. Power and domination may be coercive, but when worked through as discourse it creates willing, compliant subjects by producing and reproducing meaning. Discourses are far-reaching and pervasive. The study of discourse focuses on the detail of language but connects this to social, cultural and political meanings.

Literature reviewing

Literature reviewing is central to your research. It is through the literature that you acquire an understanding of your topic, what research has been done on it, what methods and methodologies are associated with it and what the key issues are. Literature reviewing allows you to develop understanding of the main theories around your subject and the varying ways in which it has been conceptualised, applied or changed. Through the literature you will become critically aware of the strengths and weaknesses of different theoretical stances and position yourself and your research within an ongoing debate

The purpose of a literature review is to also enable you to identify the gaps in research that your research question aims to explore.
